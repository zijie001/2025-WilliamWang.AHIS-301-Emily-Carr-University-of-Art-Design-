PRINTED PLAY

Mahjong and the Visual Culture of Games in East Asia

15th Century, Qing Dynasty

Madiao, an early card game, featured printed paper with stylized suit symbols and bamboo backing. This marked one of the earliest examples of printed gaming media in East Asia.

Late Qing Dynasty

Mahjong tiles evolved from hand-painted bone to inlaid materials like mother-of-pearl. Visual details became ornate, and material aesthetics took precedence over symbolic clarity.

1920s

Offset printing enabled widespread production of Mahjong rulebooks and packaging. Typography and visual diagrams helped standardize the game across print culture.

1930s

Tiles with English translations and illustrated guides entered Western markets. The original Chinese iconography was redrawn for clarity and cultural accessibility.

1950s

Hand-painted bone and bamboo tiles continued production. At the same time, offset-printed guides and folding rule inserts became common in boxed sets.

1980s

In diasporic zines, Mahjong iconography reappeared in risographs and screen prints. Printed textures were intentionally rough, evoking handmade memory.

2020s

Contemporary Mahjong sets emphasize minimalist digital graphics, laser etching, and on-demand print packaging—blurring tactile tradition and visual modernity.
