About

This exhibition investigates Mahjong as a printed medium, a culturally coded symbol, and an interactive game interface. By engaging with a diverse range of historical and contemporary materials—including woodblock prints, offset manuals, zine formats, and digital reinterpretations—we examine how successive print technologies have shaped the visual language, material design, and regional aesthetics of Mahjong. Through this lens, the project highlights how the game has been continually reimagined and circulated across East Asia and beyond, embedding itself within broader trajectories of graphic reproduction, leisure culture, and transnational visual exchange.

Highlights

Mahjong’s Evolution as a Visual and Cultural Object

From inked tiles to digital reinterpretations, Mahjong’s material evolution highlights the pivotal role printed forms have played in transforming traditional leisure into a dynamic visual language. This project examines how Mahjong bridges visual culture, design, and gaming, illustrating how the printed medium facilitates the mediation of interaction, identity, and cultural memory across time and space.

Key Themes

Print as Transmission

Printed Mahjong sets, rule books, and promotional materials played a pivotal role in the game’s widespread dissemination across social classes and national borders. These printed forms served not only as vehicles for gameplay but also as cultural carriers, transforming Mahjong from a localized pastime into a transnational symbol.

Design and Playability

Each era and region’s interpretation of Mahjong is shaped through its unique graphic design and physical form. These design choices—ranging from tile artwork to game packaging—profoundly affect both the mechanics of the game and its cultural perception, reinforcing its role as a socially embedded and visually distinctive object.

Global Circulation

Mahjong has traveled far beyond its origins, from Shanghai to San Francisco, carried by printed rulebooks, mass-produced sets, and repackaged versions. As Mahjong crosses cultural and geographical boundaries, it undergoes transformations that reflect not only regional aesthetics but also the ongoing exchange of cultural memory and social practice.

Visual Identity

Mahjong’s evolving iconography, from dragons to abstract geometric patterns, reflects broader shifts in cultural aesthetics and the political dynamics of play. These visual elements capture the game’s transformation from a traditional leisure activity to a symbol of modern identity, continually reshaped by the intersections of cultural politics, print technologies, and regional styles.
